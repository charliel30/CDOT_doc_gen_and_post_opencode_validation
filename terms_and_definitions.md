# Terms and Definitions

A plain-language glossary of terms used in the CDOT (Colorado Department of Transportation) highway construction procurement process. Written for someone with no prior experience in public infrastructure bidding.

---

## Project Delivery Methods

These are the different ways CDOT can structure the relationship between design and construction on a project.

### Design-Bid-Build (DBB)
The traditional method and CDOT's default. The project is designed first (by an engineering firm), then a separate construction contractor bids on the completed design. Design and construction are handled by different parties, and the contractor has no input during design. Contracts are awarded to the lowest responsive, responsible bidder.

### Construction Manager/General Contractor (CM/GC)
An alternative delivery method where a construction firm is hired *during* the design phase to advise on constructability, scheduling, and cost — then transitions to become the general contractor who builds the project. Also known as **CMGC** or **Construction Manager at Risk (CMR/CMAR)**. The "at risk" part means the contractor commits to a price ceiling and bears financial responsibility if costs exceed it. CDOT used this method for the I-70 Floyd Hill project.

### Design-Build (DB)
A single entity (usually a joint venture of a design firm and a contractor) handles both design and construction under one contract. Faster than DBB because design and construction can overlap, but the owner has less control over design details.

## Procurement Process — Step by Step

These terms follow roughly the order they appear in a real CM/GC procurement.

### Request for Qualifications (RFQ)
A document CDOT issues early in procurement asking contractors to demonstrate they are qualified to do the work. It asks about experience, key personnel, financial capacity, and past performance — but does *not* ask for a price. Used to narrow the field before requesting full proposals.

### Statement of Qualifications (SOQ)
The contractor's response to an RFQ. It details the firm's relevant experience, team, certifications, bonding capacity, and references. Sometimes called a **Statement of Interest (SOI)** when the emphasis is on expressing intent to compete rather than detailed qualifications.

### Short List
After reviewing SOQs, CDOT narrows the field to a small group (typically 3 firms) who are invited to submit full proposals. Only short-listed firms move to the RFP stage.

### Request for Proposals (RFP)
The formal solicitation document CDOT issues to short-listed firms. It specifies exactly what the proposal must contain, how it will be evaluated, and the terms of the contract. The RFP is the "rulebook" for the competition.

### Technical Proposal
The portion of a contractor's RFP response that addresses *how* they will do the work. Typically includes:
- **Project Approach**: The contractor's strategy for executing the work
- **Preliminary Plans**: Conceptual drawings (plan views, elevations, typical sections)
- **Schedule**: Phasing, milestones, and duration
- **Key Personnel**: Who will manage the project and their qualifications
- **Risk Management**: How the contractor will identify and mitigate project risks
- **Safety Plan**: Approach to worker and public safety
- **Quality Management**: How quality will be assured during construction

### Price Proposal
The portion of the RFP response that addresses cost. In CM/GC, the initial price proposal covers preconstruction services (the advisory phase), not the full construction cost — that comes later as the CAP (see below). Submitted in a separate sealed envelope from the Technical Proposal.

### Best Value Selection
The evaluation method where CDOT considers both technical quality and price, not just lowest cost. The RFP specifies the relative weight of technical vs. price factors. A firm with a higher price but significantly better technical approach can still win.

### Selection Panel
The group of CDOT staff (and sometimes external experts) who evaluate proposals, conduct interviews, and recommend award. They score proposals against the criteria published in the RFP.

## CM/GC-Specific Terms

These terms are unique to or especially important in the CM/GC delivery method.

### Preconstruction Phase
The first phase of a CM/GC contract. The contractor (acting as Construction Manager) works alongside CDOT's design team to provide input on constructability, cost estimation, scheduling, phasing, and risk identification. No dirt is moved yet — this is advisory work.

### Preconstruction Services Contract
The initial contract between CDOT and the selected CM. It covers the advisory/consulting work during design. This is a standalone contract — there is no guarantee the CM will be awarded the construction work.

### Constructability Review
A formal process where the CM evaluates the designer's plans and identifies potential construction problems, cost savings, or schedule improvements. The CM's field experience is the main value they bring during preconstruction.

### Open Book Pricing
A transparency requirement in CM/GC. The contractor must share all cost data with CDOT — subcontractor quotes, material takeoffs, labor estimates, equipment costs, and overhead calculations. CDOT can see every line item. This is the opposite of traditional bidding where cost details are proprietary.

### CAP (Construction Agreed-upon Price)
The negotiated maximum price for construction, established after the design reaches approximately 60-90% completion. In CDOT's process, this is formally called the **GC CAP** (General Contractor Construction Agreed-upon Price). If CDOT and the CM cannot agree on a CAP, CDOT can take the project to traditional competitive bidding instead.

### Guaranteed Maximum Price (GMP)
A price ceiling the contractor commits to. If actual costs come in under the GMP, savings may be shared between CDOT and the contractor. If costs exceed the GMP, the contractor absorbs the overage. The CAP and GMP are closely related concepts — the CAP is CDOT's specific term for what the industry generally calls a GMP.

### Construction Authorization
The formal decision point where CDOT authorizes the CM to transition from the advisory role to the General Contractor role and begin construction. This only happens after a CAP is successfully negotiated.

### Self-Performance Requirement
CDOT requires the CM/GC to perform a minimum percentage of the work with their own crews (not subcontractors). Typical CDOT requirements: at least **75%** of preconstruction work and at least **30%** of construction work must be self-performed.

### Independent Cost Estimate (ICE)
A cost estimate prepared by a third-party estimator (not the CM and not CDOT's design team). Used as a benchmark during CAP negotiations to verify the contractor's pricing is reasonable. Ensures neither side is negotiating blind.

### Opinion of Probable Construction Cost (OPCC)
The CM's periodic cost estimates during preconstruction, provided at various stages of design completion (e.g., 30%, 60%, 90%). These are compared against CDOT's budget and the ICE to track whether the project is staying within financial constraints.

## Bidding and Contract Terms

General terms that apply across all delivery methods.

### Prequalification
CDOT requires general contractors to be pre-approved before they can bid. The application goes through CDOT's **B2GNow** system and must be submitted at least **17 calendar days** before the bid due date. Subcontractors do not need to prequalify but must register in the system.

### Bid Express
The electronic system CDOT uses for bid submission on Design-Bid-Build projects. No paper or email bids are accepted. Contractors need a Digital ID to use the system.

### Bid Bond
A financial guarantee (typically 5% of the bid amount) that the contractor submits with their bid. If the contractor wins but refuses to sign the contract, the bid bond compensates CDOT for the difference between the winning bid and the next lowest bid. Issued by a surety company, not the contractor.

### Performance Bond
A financial guarantee (typically 100% of the contract value) that the contractor will complete the work according to the contract terms. If the contractor defaults, the surety company steps in to finish the project or pay CDOT.

### Payment Bond
A guarantee that the contractor will pay their subcontractors, suppliers, and workers. Protects those parties if the general contractor fails to pay.

### Notice to Proceed (NTP)
The formal written authorization from CDOT telling the contractor to begin work. The contract clock (and deadlines) typically starts on the NTP date, not the contract signing date.

### Change Order
A formal modification to the contract after it has been executed. Can change the scope, price, or schedule. Must be approved by both CDOT and the contractor. Common causes: unforeseen site conditions, design errors, or owner-requested changes.

### Liquidated Damages
A pre-agreed daily dollar amount the contractor owes CDOT for each day the project is late. This is not a penalty — it represents CDOT's estimated actual economic loss from the delay (traffic impacts, extended engineering oversight, etc.). The amount is specified in the contract before work begins.

### Retainage
A percentage (typically 5%) of each progress payment that CDOT withholds and holds until the project is substantially complete. Provides financial incentive for the contractor to finish all punch-list items and close out the project properly.

## Compliance and Participation

### Disadvantaged Business Enterprise (DBE)
A federal program requiring a percentage of contract dollars to go to small businesses owned by socially and economically disadvantaged individuals. CDOT sets DBE participation goals for each project. Contractors must demonstrate good-faith efforts to meet these goals, either through subcontracting or joint ventures.

### Environmental Assessment (EA)
A study required by the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) that analyzes the environmental impacts of a proposed project. Must be completed before construction can be authorized. The I-70 Floyd Hill project went through an EA process examining impacts on Clear Creek, wildlife, air quality, noise, and historic properties.

### Federal Highway Administration (FHWA)
The federal agency that oversees highway projects receiving federal funding. FHWA must approve the use of CM/GC delivery on federally funded projects. They also set rules for procurement, DBE participation, and cost eligibility.

### MAP-21
The Moving Ahead for Progress in the 21st Century Act (2012). This federal law formally authorized CM/GC as an approved delivery method for federal-aid highway projects without requiring special experimental status.

## Proposal Evaluation Ratings

When CDOT evaluates technical proposals, they typically use these rating tiers:

| Rating | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Exceptional / Outstanding** | Significantly exceeds requirements; demonstrates innovative approach with very low risk |
| **Acceptable / Satisfactory** | Meets requirements; demonstrates adequate approach with manageable risk |
| **Marginal / Deficient** | Below requirements but correctable; demonstrates approach with notable gaps or risks |
| **Unacceptable** | Fails to meet requirements; cannot be made acceptable without a complete rewrite |
