# Section-by-Section Writing Guide for CM/GC Proposals

This guide tells OpenCode **how** to write each section of the proposal — what tone to use, what to emphasize, what to avoid, and how to tie content back to the source documents.

---

## General Principles

- **Write as the contractor**, not as CDOT. The voice is a construction firm explaining why they are the best choice.
- **Be specific to the project.** Every claim should reference the actual project scope, site conditions, or constraints from the source documents. Generic statements score poorly.
- **Show, don't tell.** Instead of "we have extensive experience," cite specific past projects with measurable outcomes.
- **Use the correct terminology.** Refer to `terms_and_definitions.md` for proper CDOT/CM/GC vocabulary.
- **Stay within page limits.** Be concise. Evaluators read many proposals — clarity and density win over length.

---

## Cover Letter

**Purpose:** Formality. Sets a professional tone.

**How to write it:**
- Keep it to exactly 1 page
- Open with: "[Firm Name] is pleased to submit this proposal in response to CDOT's Request for Proposals for [Project Name], Project No. [Number]."
- Middle paragraph: 2–3 sentences on why the firm is qualified (biggest differentiator only)
- Close with: authorized signatory name, title, contact info
- Do NOT repeat the executive summary here

---

## Section 1: Executive Summary

**Purpose:** Give evaluators the "elevator pitch" in 2 minutes of reading.

**How to write it:**
- First paragraph: Who we are and our single strongest qualification for this project
- Second paragraph: Our approach in plain language — what we plan to do and how
- Third paragraph: 2–3 specific innovations or differentiators (not generic claims)
- Final sentence: Commitment statement to CDOT's project goals (reference the actual goals from source docs)

**What to avoid:**
- Don't summarize the entire proposal section by section
- Don't use superlatives without evidence ("world-class," "unparalleled")
- Don't exceed 3 pages

## Section 2: Key Personnel and Organization

**Purpose:** Prove the team is experienced, committed, and well-structured.

**How to write it:**

*Organizational Chart:*
- Put the Project Manager at the top
- Show CDOT's team on one side to illustrate the interface
- Keep it on one page — legibility over detail
- Use boxes with Name + Title + Years of Experience

*Resumes:*
- Lead with the person's proposed role on THIS project
- List 3–5 past projects that are most similar to this one
- For each past project: owner name, dollar value, scope in one sentence, their role
- Include certifications (PE, PMP, OSHA 30, CCM) and education
- State percentage of time committed to this project

*What scores well:*
- Personnel with direct CDOT CM/GC experience
- Personnel with experience on mountain corridor or steep-grade projects
- High percentage of time dedicated (80%+ for PM and Superintendent)
- Stable team with low turnover risk

**What to avoid:**
- Listing personnel who won't actually work on the project
- Vague availability ("as needed")
- Resumes that read like LinkedIn profiles instead of project histories

---

## Section 3: Project Understanding

**Purpose:** Prove the contractor has deeply studied this specific project and understands what makes it hard.

**How to write it:**
- Open with the project's purpose and why it matters (congestion, safety, economic corridor)
- Identify 5–7 key challenges, drawn directly from the source documents:
  - Steep grades (7% profile, 600-foot descent in 2 miles)
  - Rock excavation volumes (600,000+ tons)
  - Segmental bridge construction over live I-70 traffic
  - Clear Creek environmental protections (water quality, wildlife)
  - Construction phasing across 3 geographic sections simultaneously
  - Weekend/seasonal traffic surges (ski traffic)
  - Stakeholder complexity (County, Idaho Springs, FHWA, watershed groups)
- For each challenge, briefly note why it matters — don't solve it here (that's Section 4)

**What scores well:**
- Referencing specific project data (mile markers, elevation changes, tonnage, creek crossings)
- Mentioning environmental permits and mitigation commitments by name
- Showing awareness of stakeholder concerns beyond CDOT

**What to avoid:**
- Restating the RFP scope verbatim
- Generic challenges that apply to any highway project
- Solving problems here (save solutions for Project Approach)

---

## Section 4: Project Approach

**Purpose:** Show HOW the contractor will execute the work, both during preconstruction and construction.

**How to write it:**

*Preconstruction:*
- Describe the collaboration model with CDOT's design team
- Explain how constructability reviews will work (frequency, deliverables, decision process)
- Detail the cost estimating methodology — how OPCCs will be developed and compared to the ICE
- Describe value engineering: how ideas are generated, evaluated, and presented to CDOT

*Construction:*
- For each major scope element (bridges, roadway realignment, interchanges, retaining walls, wildlife crossings), describe the proposed means and methods
- Explain the phasing strategy: which sections are built first and why
- Detail the traffic management approach: how I-70 stays open during construction, detour plans, nightwork strategy
- Address rock blasting: sequencing, safety protocols, traffic holds

*Subcontractor Management:*
- Name types of specialty subcontractors needed (not specific firms at this stage)
- Describe the selection and oversight process
- Address the 30% self-performance requirement: what work will be self-performed

**What scores well:**
- Detailed, project-specific means and methods (not "we will use appropriate equipment")
- A phasing strategy that shows understanding of the 3-section geographic approach
- Traffic management that addresses both weekday and weekend/ski traffic patterns
- References to lessons learned from similar projects

**What to avoid:**
- Generic descriptions that could apply to any highway project
- Promising outcomes without explaining how
- Ignoring environmental constraints in the construction approach

## Section 5: Schedule

**Purpose:** Demonstrate a realistic, well-sequenced plan that meets CDOT's milestones.

**How to write it:**
- Present as a summary CPM (Critical Path Method) or Gantt chart
- Show both preconstruction and construction phases
- Align milestones with CDOT's published targets (e.g., WB I-70 new alignment by end of 2027)
- Identify the critical path and explain why those activities drive the schedule
- Call out seasonal constraints: winter weather, peak ski traffic, environmental windows
- Include a schedule risk section: what could delay the critical path and how you'd respond

**What scores well:**
- Milestones that match or beat CDOT's targets with a credible explanation
- Built-in float for high-risk activities (blasting, bridge erection, winter weather)
- Showing awareness of the parallel 3-section construction approach

**What to avoid:**
- Unrealistically aggressive schedules with no contingency
- Schedules that ignore seasonal weather constraints
- Missing critical milestones from the project specs

---

## Section 6: Risk Management

**Purpose:** Show that the contractor can identify, quantify, and mitigate risks proactively.

**How to write it:**
- Present risks in a table format: Risk | Category | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation
- Organize by category (geotechnical, environmental, traffic, cost, schedule, design)
- For each risk, the mitigation must be specific and actionable — not "we will monitor"
- Describe the risk register process: how risks are tracked, updated, and communicated to CDOT
- Include an escalation protocol: when and how CDOT is alerted to emerging risks

**Example risks for Floyd Hill:**
| Risk | Category | Mitigation |
|------|----------|------------|
| Unexpected rock conditions during blasting | Geotechnical | Pre-blast geotechnical surveys; adaptive blast design; contingency quantities in CAP |
| Sediment runoff into Clear Creek during earthwork | Environmental | Erosion control plan per SWEEP ITF requirements; real-time turbidity monitoring |
| Weekend ski traffic conflicts with lane closures | Traffic | No closures Friday PM–Sunday PM during ski season; nightwork for critical crossings |
| Material cost escalation between preconstruction and CAP | Cost | Early procurement of long-lead items; commodity price tracking; escalation allowances |

## Section 7: Safety Plan

**Purpose:** Demonstrate a proactive safety culture, not just compliance.

**How to write it:**
- Lead with the firm's safety record (EMR, TRIR, DART rates for past 3 years)
- Identify this project's specific hazards: steep grades, live traffic, blasting, heavy equipment near creek
- Describe training: OSHA 30 for supervisors, OSHA 10 for all workers, project-specific orientation
- Include public safety measures: how motorists and pedestrians are protected
- Describe incident reporting and investigation procedures

---

## Section 8: Quality Management

**Purpose:** Show defined processes for ensuring work meets specs.

**How to write it:**
- Describe the QA/QC organizational structure (who is responsible, reporting independence)
- List inspection and testing protocols for major items (concrete, asphalt, structural steel, earthwork)
- Describe documentation: daily reports, inspection logs, test results, non-conformance reports
- Explain the resolution process when non-conforming work is found

---

## Section 9: Innovation and Value Engineering

**Purpose:** Demonstrate creative thinking that can save cost, time, or improve quality.

**How to write it:**
- Propose 2–4 specific ideas (not vague "we will look for savings")
- For each idea: describe it, estimate the benefit (cost, schedule, or quality), and explain feasibility
- Reference past projects where similar innovations were successfully implemented

**Example ideas for Floyd Hill:**
- Advanced formwork systems for segmental bridge to accelerate casting cycle
- Drone-based survey and progress monitoring to reduce manual inspection time
- BIM coordination for utility conflicts and retaining wall design optimization
- Precast concrete elements for retaining walls to reduce on-site forming time

---

## Section 10: Price Proposal

**Purpose:** Provide a competitive and transparent fee structure.

**How to write it:**
- Preconstruction fee: present as a lump sum with a breakdown by task category
- CM/GC management fee: present as a percentage with a clear statement of what is included/excluded
- Personnel rates: table format with name, role, hourly rate, estimated hours
- State all assumptions clearly

**What to avoid:**
- Artificially low fees that suggest the contractor doesn't understand the scope
- Missing assumptions (evaluators will assume the worst)
- Fees that are inconsistent with the proposed staffing plan
