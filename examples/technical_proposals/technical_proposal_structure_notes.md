# Technical Proposal Structure — Key Observations Across Examples

This document summarizes the patterns observed across the two example CM/GC proposals (Floyd Hill and Vail Pass) to help OpenCode understand what is consistent vs. project-specific.

---

## Consistent Structure Across All CM/GC Technical Proposals

Every CM/GC technical proposal follows the same skeleton regardless of project:

1. **Cover Letter** — 1 page, formal, signed by authorized officer
2. **Executive Summary** — 2-3 pages, elevator pitch, 2-3 differentiators
3. **Key Personnel and Organization** — Org chart + resumes + availability statement
4. **Project Understanding** — What makes THIS project hard (5-7 challenges)
5. **Project Approach** — How to solve it (preconstruction + construction + subs)
6. **Schedule** — Preconstruction + construction timelines with milestones
7. **Risk Management** — Risk table + mitigation + tracking process
8. **Safety Plan** — Safety record + project-specific hazards + program description
9. **Quality Management** — QA/QC org + testing protocols + NCR process
10. **Innovation** — 2-4 specific ideas with benefits and feasibility

This structure maps directly to the evaluation criteria in the RFP.

## What Changes Between Projects

| Element | Floyd Hill Example | Vail Pass Example | Pattern |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|---------|
| Key challenges | Segmental bridges, rock blasting, Clear Creek | High altitude, short season, avalanche zones | Always project-specific |
| Construction means/methods | Cast-in-place segmental, massive blasting | GPS machine control, modular creek crossings | Driven by scope |
| Schedule constraints | 3-section concurrent construction | 6-month construction season at altitude | Driven by geography/climate |
| Environmental focus | Clear Creek water quality, SWEEP ITF | Black Gore Creek Gold Medal fishery | Driven by site ecology |
| Traffic concerns | Ski traffic on I-70 mountain corridor | Ski traffic on Vail Pass | Similar corridor = similar concerns |
| Innovations | Traveling formwork, real-time water monitoring | Modular creek bridges, ground-thaw mats | Must be relevant to THIS project |
| Personnel | Mountain highway + bridge specialists | High-altitude + winter construction specialists | Matched to project demands |
| Safety hazards | Blasting near traffic, overhead bridge work | Avalanche, altitude sickness, winter exposure | Driven by site conditions |

## What Makes a Strong Technical Proposal

Based on evaluation criteria and the examples:

### DO:
- Reference specific project data (mile markers, tonnage, elevation, creek names)
- Show that you've studied the FONSI/EA and environmental commitments
- Name actual stakeholders and their specific concerns
- Provide measurable benefits for innovations ("4 months savings" not "significant savings")
- Include past project examples with dollar values and outcomes
- Show personnel dedicated at high percentages (80-100%)
- Build schedule around known seasonal and traffic constraints

### DON'T:
- Use boilerplate language that could apply to any project
- Claim innovations without evidence of past success
- Propose unrealistic schedules with no contingency
- List personnel who are overcommitted to other projects
- Ignore environmental constraints in the construction approach
- Exceed page limits or violate formatting requirements
- Solve problems in the Project Understanding section (save for Project Approach)

## Evaluation Weight Patterns

Based on typical CDOT CM/GC RFPs:

| Factor | Typical Weight Range | Notes |
|--------|---------------------|-------|
| Technical (total) | 65-75% | Higher for complex projects |
| Price (total) | 15-25% | Lower weight = quality matters more than cost |
| Interview | 5-15% | Tests team chemistry and depth |

Within technical:
| Factor | Typical Weight Range |
|--------|---------------------|
| Key Personnel | 15-25% |
| Project Approach | 15-25% |
| Project Understanding | 8-15% |
| Schedule | 8-12% |
| Risk/Safety/Quality | 5-8% each |
| Innovation | 3-5% |
