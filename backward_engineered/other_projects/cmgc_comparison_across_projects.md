## Common Themes Across All CDOT CM/GC Projects

### Why CDOT Chooses CM/GC
Based on CDOT's published rationale for selecting CM/GC on various projects:

1. **Complexity** — Projects with unusual construction challenges benefit from contractor input during design
2. **Risk sharing** — CM/GC distributes risk between owner and contractor through collaborative design
3. **Schedule flexibility** — CM/GC allows phased construction, early work packages, and adaptive scheduling
4. **Funding uncertainty** — CM/GC accommodates phased funding better than lump-sum design-build

CDOT's stated rationale for Floyd Hill specifically: CM/GC "has significant benefits for risk management and risk sharing with complex projects, provides the best schedule and scope flexibility given the complexity of the project and likely phasing of funding."

### Common Proposal Elements
Across all CDOT CM/GC solicitations, proposals consistently require:

- Two-envelope submission (technical + price)
- Key personnel with specific CM/GC experience
- Project-specific approach (not generic boilerplate)
- Preliminary schedule with milestones
- Risk register with mitigation strategies
- Safety plan with quantified safety record
- Self-performance commitment (75% precon / 30% construction)
- DBE participation plan

### Common Evaluation Patterns
- Technical quality always weighted higher than price (typically 70/20/10 split)
- Best value, not lowest price
- Interview component rewards team chemistry and communication skills
- Past CDOT CM/GC experience is strongly favored (but not required)

## Project-Specific Differentiators

### What Makes Each Project's Proposal Unique

| Project Type | Key Differentiator in Proposal |
|-------------|-------------------------------|
| Mountain corridor (Floyd Hill, Vail Pass) | Terrain challenges, weather constraints, environmental sensitivity, ski traffic |
| Urban interstate (I-25 Segment 5, Vasquez Blvd.) | Utility relocations, high traffic volumes, property impacts, business access |
| Bridge replacement (I-270 Critical Bridges, US 6 Elk Creek) | Structural engineering approach, over-water work, temporary detours |
| Transit/multimodal (Federal Blvd. BRT, CO 119) | Stakeholder coordination, transit operations during construction, pedestrian safety |

### The Pattern for Proposers
1. **Read the RFP carefully** — every project has unique evaluation criteria and weights
2. **Study the site** — the most valued proposals demonstrate first-hand site knowledge
3. **Match your team to the challenges** — don't propose a bridge specialist for a transit project
4. **Reference comparable experience** — the closer your past projects are to this one, the better
5. **Be specific about innovations** — generic "we'll use BIM" scores lower than "we used BIM on [specific project] and reduced RFIs by 30%"

## Lessons Learned from CDOT CM/GC Delivery

Based on publicly reported outcomes:

### What Has Worked Well
- **Floyd Hill:** CM/GC allowed Kraemer to influence bridge design during preconstruction, optimizing the segmental approach and identifying rock excavation efficiencies before the CAP was set
- **I-25 projects:** CM/GC enabled early work packages to proceed before full design was complete, accelerating overall schedule
- **Veterans Memorial Tunnels:** Kraemer's CM/GC approach on this earlier project built institutional knowledge that carried over to Floyd Hill

### Common Challenges
- **Cost growth:** Floyd Hill grew from $700M (2020) to $905M (2025) — market conditions can outpace even CM/GC cost management
- **CAP timing:** Negotiating the CAP requires design to be sufficiently complete; if design is delayed, CAP negotiation is delayed
- **Public perception:** The public doesn't always understand why CM/GC costs more than the original estimate when the point was to control costs
