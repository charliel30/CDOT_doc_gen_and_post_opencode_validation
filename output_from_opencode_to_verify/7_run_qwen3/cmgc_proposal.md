## Section 3: Project Understanding

The I-70 Floyd Hill project addresses one of Colorado's most critical transportation bottlenecks — an 8-mile stretch of I-70 through Clear Creek County where westbound traffic climbs nearly 2,000 feet of elevation to the top of Floyd Hill, then descends 600 feet in less than 2 miles to the I-70/US 6 interchange. This steep grade, combined with tight curves (existing bridges rated for only 45 mph) and heavy ski/recreation traffic, creates recurring congestion, safety hazards, and unreliable travel times that impact the state's economy and quality of life.

We have identified the following key challenges that will drive our approach:

**1. Segmental Bridge Construction Over Live Traffic**
The project includes over 10,000 feet of new bridges, with the largest exceeding one mile in length. A new 115-foot tall bridge will carry westbound traffic in a shifted alignment above Clear Creek. Cast-in-place segmental construction with balanced cantilever methods will require precise execution over active I-70 traffic lanes. This is the project's highest-risk construction element.

**2. Massive Rock Excavation in Constrained Corridor**
Removing hundreds of thousands of tons of rock from hillsides above I-70 requires controlled blasting in close proximity to live traffic, existing structures, Clear Creek, and residential areas. Blast design must balance production rates with safety and vibration limits. The 7% profile grade on the east section adds drainage and access complexity during excavation.

**3. Clear Creek Environmental Protections**
Clear Creek runs through the entire 8-mile corridor. The FONSI and 1041 permit impose strict requirements for water quality protection, sediment control, and creek/riparian restoration. The SWEEP ITF actively monitors sediment and chloride impacts. Any spill or sediment release into Clear Creek would have immediate regulatory and public relations consequences.

**4. Wildlife Connectivity**
Wildlife crossings at Genesee and Empire, 2 miles of deer fencing, 7 escape ramps, and a deer guard on CR 65 must be constructed while maintaining wildlife movement during the multi-year construction period.

**5. Three-Section Concurrent Construction**
The East (July 2023–Summer 2026), Central (Fall 2024–Late 2028), and West (Spring 2024–Summer 2028) sections will be under construction simultaneously. Managing three active work zones in an 8-mile corridor on a two-lane mountain interstate requires precise coordination of resources, traffic control, and material deliveries.

**6. Traffic Management on Colorado's Primary Mountain Corridor**
I-70 is the only east-west interstate through the Colorado mountains. Weekend ski traffic, summer recreation traffic, and local Idaho Springs/Central City traffic must be accommodated throughout construction. Public tolerance for delays is low, and CDOT's reputation depends on keeping I-70 moving.

**7. Multi-Stakeholder Coordination**
Clear Creek County (1041 permit authority), Idaho Springs, FHWA, the Upper Clear Creek Watershed Association, Colorado Parks and Wildlife, the SWEEP ITF, and the I-70 Coalition all have active interests in this project. Successful delivery requires proactive communication and responsiveness to each stakeholder's concerns.

## Section 4: Project Approach

### 4.1 Preconstruction Approach

**Collaborative Design Integration**
We will co-locate a preconstruction team at CDOT's project office, including our Estimating Manager and a constructability lead, to provide daily interaction with the AtkinsRealis design team. Weekly constructability review meetings will be supplemented by real-time informal consultation.

**Constructability Review Process**
At each design milestone (30%, 60%, 90%), we will deliver a formal Constructability Review Report identifying:
- Construction sequencing conflicts
- Access and staging challenges
- Opportunities to simplify construction without compromising design intent
- Material or method substitutions that reduce cost or schedule

**Cost Estimating**
Lisa Nguyen will develop OPCCs at 30%, 60%, and 90% design using our proprietary estimating database calibrated to Colorado mountain construction. Each OPCC will include:
- Detailed quantity takeoffs by work item
- Unit pricing based on current market conditions and subcontractor input
- Risk-based contingency allocation
- Comparison to CDOT's budget and the ICE

**Value Engineering**
We will present formal VE proposals at the 30% and 60% milestones, with each proposal including a cost-benefit analysis and constructability assessment. Our preliminary VE ideas include:
- Accelerated segmental bridge casting cycle using traveling formwork (estimated 4-month schedule savings)
- Precast retaining wall elements to reduce on-site forming (estimated 8% cost reduction on walls)
- Optimized blast patterns based on pre-construction geotechnical investigation (estimated 12% reduction in over-break)

### 4.2 Construction Approach

**Segmental Bridge Construction**
We propose cast-in-place segmental construction using a traveling formwork system with a 7-day casting cycle per segment. Our team has completed 4 segmental bridge projects in mountain terrain. For the mile-long westbound bridge, we will use two casting crews working from multiple piers simultaneously to parallelize the critical path. All overhead work will be performed during nighttime I-70 closures (9 PM–5 AM) with pre-positioned safety nets and debris containment systems.

**Rock Excavation and Blasting**
Jeff Carpenter has supervised over 200 controlled blasts on mountain highway projects. Our approach:
- Pre-blast geotechnical surveys (core samples every 50 feet) to map rock quality and fracture patterns
- Electronic detonator systems for precise blast timing and vibration control
- Blast monitoring stations at 100-foot intervals along I-70 and at Clear Creek
- All blasting during weekday off-peak hours with 15-minute traffic holds
- Muck hauling via dedicated haul routes separated from public traffic

**Phasing Strategy**
| Section | Primary Work | Sequence |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| East | Rock excavation, roadway construction, initial bridge work | Start first (July 2023) — most construction access, least traffic impact |
| West | Interchange improvements, frontage road, wildlife crossings | Start second (Spring 2024) — enables early delivery of resiliency benefits |
| Central | Major bridge construction, highway realignment | Start third (Fall 2024) — requires longest duration, most complex work |

This sequence front-loads work in areas where construction access is best and traffic impacts are lowest, then transitions to the Central section where the major bridge work is concentrated.

**Traffic Management**
- Zero lane closures on Friday PM through Sunday PM during ski season (November–April)
- Nighttime-only closures for overhead bridge work (9 PM–5 AM)
- Dedicated pilot car operations during single-lane segments
- Real-time travel time displays at corridor entry points
- Coordination with CDOT's text alert system ("floydhill" to 21000)
- Courtesy shuttle service for Idaho Springs residents and workers during extended closures

### 4.3 Subcontractor Management
We will self-perform a minimum of 35% of construction work (exceeding the 30% requirement), including all rock excavation, earthwork, and concrete bridge work. Specialty subcontractors will be engaged for:
- Electrical and ITS systems
- Landscaping and revegetation
- Wildlife crossing structures
- Drainage pipe installation
- Asphalt paving

All subcontractors will be pre-qualified through our internal vetting process and must meet our safety and quality standards. We are committed to exceeding CDOT's DBE participation goals.
