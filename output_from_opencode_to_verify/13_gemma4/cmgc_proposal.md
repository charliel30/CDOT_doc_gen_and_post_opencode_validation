## Section 3: Project Understanding

The I-70 Floyd Hill to Veterans Memorial Tunnels Improvements project is critical to resolving one of the most persistent bottlenecks in Colorado's primary mountain corridor. Spanning approximately 8 miles from County Road 65 to Colorado Boulevard, the project addresses a volatile segment of the interstate where westbound traffic climbs nearly 2,000 feet to the summit of Floyd Hill before descending 600 feet in less than 2 miles. The combination of steep grades, tight curves currently rated for only 45 mph, and immense seasonal traffic surges creates a high-risk environment for safety and reliability. Our objective is to transform this corridor into a modern, high-capacity facility with a design speed of 55 mph, ensuring safer and more predictable travel for the motoring public.

Based on our analysis of the project specifications and site conditions, we have identified the following key challenges that will drive our execution strategy:

**1. Massive Rock Excavation and Blasting in a Constrained Corridor**
The project requires the removal of over 600,000 tons of material, including 97,000 tons from the hillside above eastbound I-70 in the East Section alone. Executing 135+ blasts in close proximity to live traffic, residential areas, and the Clear Creek watershed requires an extremely precise blast design to balance production rates with vibration limits and public safety.

**2. Segmental Bridge Construction Over Live Traffic**
The delivery of over 10,000 feet of new bridges—including a centerpiece structure exceeding one mile in length and a 115-foot tall bridge carrying westbound traffic—represents the project's highest technical risk. Utilizing cast-in-place segmental construction with balanced cantilever methods over active I-70 lanes requires rigorous sequencing and debris containment to prevent any impact on the traveling public.

**3. Extreme Topography and Drainage Engineering**
The 7% profile grade in the East Section creates significant challenges for drainage and erosion control. Managing stormwater runoff on such steep terrain, while preventing sediment from entering the watershed, will require integrated drainage solutions and staged grading to maintain stability throughout the construction lifecycle.

**4. Absolute Protection of the Clear Creek Watershed**
Clear Creek runs through the entire 8-mile project corridor, making water quality protection a non-negotiable priority. We are acutely aware of the requirements set by the SWEEP ITF and the Clear Creek County 1041 permit. Any failure in sediment control or a chloride release would have immediate regulatory consequences and impact the riparian habitats of the valley.

**5. Complex Wildlife Connectivity and Mitigation**
Integrating two wildlife crossings at Genesee and Empire, 2 miles of deer fencing, and 7 escape ramps requires careful coordination with Colorado Parks and Wildlife. These elements must be constructed without compromising the project schedule, while ensuring the 90% reduction in wildlife-vehicle collisions target is achieved.

**6. Three-Section Concurrent Construction Phasing**
Managing the East (starting July 2023), West (starting Spring 2024), and Central (starting Fall 2024) sections simultaneously in an 8-mile corridor is a massive logistical undertaking. This requires a sophisticated resource mobilization plan to prevent equipment conflicts and ensure that the Westbound alignment is delivered by the end of 2027.

**7. High-Stakes Stakeholder Coordination**
The project involves a complex web of stakeholders, including FHWA, the City of Idaho Springs, Clear Creek County (the 1041 permit authority), and the Upper Clear Creek Watershed Association. Success depends on a proactive communication strategy that addresses the specific concerns of each entity, from federal compliance to local community impacts.

## Section 4: Project Approach

### 4.1 Preconstruction Approach

Kraemer North America views the preconstruction phase not as a formality, but as the primary mechanism for risk elimination. Our approach is built on a foundation of deep collaboration and transparency.

**Collaborative Design Integration**
We will implement a co-location model, embedding our Project Manager and Estimating Manager within CDOT's project office. This allows for real-time, informal coordination with the AtkinsRealis design team, reducing the RFI cycle and ensuring that constructability is baked into the design from the outset.

**Constructability Review Process**
At the 30%, 60%, and 90% design milestones, Kraemer will deliver formal Constructability Review Reports. These reports will not merely identify problems but will provide specific, actionable alternatives to reduce cost and schedule risk. Our focus will be on the high-risk elements: the 115-foot tall shifted alignment bridge and the massive rock excavation in the East Section.

**Cost Estimating and OPCC Development**
Lisa Nguyen will lead the development of the Opinion of Probable Construction Cost (OPCC) at each milestone. We utilize a proprietary estimating database calibrated to current Colorado mountain corridor costs, ensuring that our estimates reflect real-world market conditions. Each OPCC will be developed using open-book principles, providing CDOT with full visibility into quantity takeoffs and unit pricing, which will be compared against the Independent Cost Estimate (ICE) to ensure budget alignment.

**Value Engineering (VE)**
Our VE process focuses on "functional equivalence"—finding ways to achieve the project's goals more efficiently. We will present formal VE proposals at the 30% and 60% milestones, focusing on areas such as optimizing blast patterns to reduce over-break and exploring precast elements for the 4 miles of retaining walls to accelerate the schedule.

### 4.2 Construction Approach

Our construction strategy is designed to maintain the mobility of I-70 while executing high-complexity work in a constrained mountain corridor.

**Major Work Elements: Means and Methods**
- **Segmental Bridge Construction:** For the mile-long westbound bridge, we will utilize cast-in-place segmental construction with a balanced cantilever method. To parallelize the critical path, we will deploy multiple casting crews working from different piers simultaneously. All overhead work will be strictly limited to nighttime closures (9 PM–5 AM) using debris containment systems to protect active traffic.
- **Rock Excavation and Blasting:** We will employ controlled blasting using electronic detonators for precision timing and vibration control. To minimize impact, we will implement a strict sequence of pre-blast geotechnical surveys and real-time monitoring at 100-foot intervals. Blasting will be scheduled during weekday off-peak hours with coordinated 15-minute traffic holds.
- **Retaining Walls and Shotcrete:** For the 73,000 square feet of shotcrete, we will use advanced application techniques to ensure the required natural-looking finish while maintaining structural reinforcement.

**Phasing and Sequencing**
We will execute a parallel three-section approach to optimize resource utilization:
- **East Section:** Priority focus on rock excavation and roadway construction to establish early access.
- **West Section:** Focus on interchange improvements and wildlife crossings to deliver early resiliency benefits.
- **Central Section:** Concentrated effort on the major bridge structures and highway realignment.

**Traffic Management and Corridor Mobility**
Recognizing that I-70 is the primary mountain artery, we will implement a "Zero-Impact" strategy during peak periods:
- **Ski Season Protection:** No lane closures from Friday PM through Sunday PM during the November–April window.
- **Night-Shift Priority:** All critical overhead bridge work and high-risk blasting will be shifted to nighttime or off-peak windows.
- **Real-Time Communication:** We will integrate with CDOT's text alert system ("floydhill" to 21000) and provide courtesy shuttle services for Idaho Springs residents to mitigate local congestion.

### 4.3 Subcontractor Management

**Selection and Oversight**
Kraemer will utilize a rigorous vetting process for specialty subcontractors, requiring evidence of experience in mountain corridor work and a proven safety record. We will maintain a dedicated subcontractor coordinator to manage the 162+ firms required for this project, ensuring a seamless interface between specialty trades and the general contractor.

**Self-Performance Commitment**
Kraemer is committed to exceeding CDOT's 30% self-performance requirement. We will self-perform all core heavy civil work, including:
- All rock excavation and blasting.
- All earthwork and grading.
- All concrete bridge and retaining wall construction.
This high level of self-performance ensures direct control over the project's most critical risks and quality outcomes.

**DBE Participation and Local Engagement**
We have a demonstrated track record of exceeding CDOT's DBE goals. On the Floyd Hill project, we have already engaged 152 Colorado-based businesses. We will continue this commitment through an active outreach program and mentoring partnerships, ensuring that the project's economic benefits remain within the state.

- **Material Lead Times:** All long-lead items (structural steel, bearings) will be procured within 90 days of construction NTP to mitigate supply chain disruption.

## Section 9: Innovation and Value Engineering

Kraemer North America does not apply generic solutions to unique problems. Our approach to innovation is focused on delivering measurable improvements in cost, schedule, and quality through the application of proven technologies and creative engineering.

We propose the following project-specific innovations for the Floyd Hill project:

**1. Accelerated Segmental Bridge Casting Cycle**
To mitigate the schedule risk associated with the mile-long westbound bridge, we propose the use of an advanced traveling formwork system with integrated heated enclosures. This technology allows for a consistent 7-day casting cycle, regardless of mountain weather conditions, potentially reducing the bridge construction schedule by several months. This method has been successfully deployed by Kraemer on previous CDOT mountain corridor projects.

**2. Real-Time Water Quality Monitoring Network**
To ensure absolute compliance with the SWEEP ITF and Clear Creek County 1041 permit, we propose deploying a network of continuous turbidity, pH, and conductivity sensors at critical points along Clear Creek. This system provides real-time alerts to our environmental team and CDOT, allowing for immediate response to sediment events and providing a transparent, digital record of compliance that exceeds traditional manual sampling.

**3. BIM-Integrated 4D Sequencing**
We will utilize Building Information Modeling (BIM) linked directly to our CPM schedule. This 4D simulation allows the team to visually "build" the project in virtual space, identifying potential utility conflicts and spatial constraints in the Central Section before they manifest in the field. This approach significantly reduces the volume of RFIs and prevents costly field re-work.

**4. Precast Retaining Wall Optimization**
Where geotechnical conditions permit, we propose replacing conventional cast-in-place retaining walls with high-performance precast panels. By shifting production to a controlled factory environment, we can improve quality consistency, reduce on-site forming labor, and accelerate the wall installation schedule, particularly in the East Section.
