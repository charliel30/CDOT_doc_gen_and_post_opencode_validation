# CM/GC Proposal Evaluation Criteria

This document describes how CDOT evaluates CM/GC proposals, combining CDOT's published evaluation factors with federal best-value selection guidance.

---

## CDOT's 8 Project Delivery Evaluation Factors

CDOT uses a risk-based Project Delivery Selection Matrix (PDSM) to evaluate CM/GC projects. The 8 evaluation factors are:

### 1. Delivery Schedule
- Can the contractor meet critical milestones and final completion dates?
- Is the proposed phasing plan realistic and well-sequenced?
- Does the schedule account for seasonal constraints (e.g., mountain weather, ski traffic)?
- Are contingency periods included for high-risk activities?

### 2. Project Complexity and Innovation
- Does the contractor understand the unique technical challenges of the project?
- Are innovative solutions proposed for difficult construction elements?
- Does the approach demonstrate experience with similar complexity (e.g., segmental bridges, steep grades, active traffic)?

### 3. Level of Design
- How well does the contractor's approach integrate with the current design stage?
- Can the contractor provide meaningful constructability input at this design completion level?
- Does the proposal show understanding of design gaps and how to address them?

### 4. Initial Project Risk
- Has the contractor identified the major risks (geotechnical, environmental, traffic, schedule, cost)?
- Are the proposed mitigation strategies specific and actionable?
- Does the risk assessment align with CDOT's own risk register?

### 5. Cost
- Is the preconstruction fee reasonable for the scope of advisory services?
- Is the CM/GC management fee percentage competitive?
- Does the cost approach demonstrate commitment to cost control during construction?
- How does the proposed cost compare to the Independent Cost Estimate?

### 6. Staff Experience and Availability
- Do key personnel have relevant CM/GC experience on similar projects?
- Are proposed personnel committed to this project (not split across many other projects)?
- Does the team have the right mix of skills (estimating, scheduling, constructability, safety)?

### 7. Level of Oversight and Control
- How will the contractor manage subcontractors and suppliers?
- What quality assurance/quality control procedures are proposed?
- How will the contractor ensure compliance with environmental permits and mitigation commitments?
- What reporting and communication protocols are proposed?

### 8. Competition and Contractor Experience
- Does the contractor have a track record of successful CM/GC project delivery?
- Has the contractor worked on CDOT projects before?
- Can the contractor demonstrate successful open-book negotiations on past projects?
- Is the contractor's bonding capacity adequate for the project scale?

## Evaluation Process

### Technical Evaluation (Envelope 1)

The Selection Panel evaluates each technical proposal section independently:

| Section | What Evaluators Look For |
|---------|------------------------|
| Key Personnel | Relevant experience, availability, stability of team |
| Organizational Chart | Clear reporting lines, adequate staffing, logical structure |
| Project Understanding | Depth of knowledge about project challenges and context |
| Project Approach | Feasibility, innovation, alignment with CDOT goals |
| Schedule | Realism, completeness, risk mitigation built into timeline |
| Risk Management | Comprehensive risk identification, specific mitigation strategies |
| Safety | Proactive safety culture, specific safety plans for high-risk activities |
| Quality Management | Defined QA/QC processes, inspection protocols, documentation |

### Rating Scale

CDOT uses a **Modified Satisficing** approach with a color-coded rating system:

| Color | Rating | Description |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| **Green** | Acceptable | Response shows significant strengths and/or minor strengths with no significant weaknesses |
| **Yellow** | Marginal | Reasonably susceptible to becoming acceptable; fails to adequately satisfy standards but weaknesses can be corrected through exchanges |
| **Red** | Unsatisfactory | Fails to meet stated criteria; not capable of becoming acceptable without major revisions |

Some RFPs also use a 5-point numeric scale:

| Rating | Points | Description |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| Exceptional | 5 | Significantly exceeds requirements; innovative, very low risk |
| Good | 4 | Exceeds requirements in meaningful ways; low risk |
| Acceptable | 3 | Meets all requirements; manageable risk |
| Marginal | 2 | Below requirements but correctable; notable gaps |
| Unacceptable | 1 | Fails to meet minimum requirements; disqualifying if not corrected |

**Minimum threshold:** Proposals scoring below approximately 70% of possible technical points may be eliminated before price evaluation.

### Price Evaluation (Envelope 2)

Price proposals are scored separately, typically using a formula such as:

```
Price Score = (Lowest Price / Proposer's Price) × Maximum Price Points
```

### Best Value Calculation

The final ranking combines technical and price scores:

```
Total Score = Technical Score + Price Score
```

The relative weight of technical vs. price varies by project and is always stated in the RFP. For complex projects (like Floyd Hill), technical factors typically carry more weight than price.

### Interview

Short-listed firms typically present to the Selection Panel:
- 30–60 minute presentation
- Key personnel must attend (substitutions may be penalized)
- Panel asks questions about the proposal and the team's approach
- Interview scores may be incorporated into the technical score or scored separately

## What Makes a Proposal Score Well

Based on published evaluation criteria and industry best practices:

### High-Scoring Proposals
- **Specific to this project** — not generic boilerplate reused from other proposals
- **Risk-aware** — identifies risks the owner cares about and offers concrete mitigation
- **Realistic schedule** — shows understanding of constraints (weather, traffic, environmental windows)
- **Strong personnel** — experienced people committed full-time, not spread across projects
- **Innovation** — proposes value engineering or novel construction methods that save cost or schedule
- **Clear communication** — well-organized, concise, easy for evaluators to find information

### Low-Scoring Proposals
- Generic approach with no project-specific detail
- Unrealistic schedule that ignores known constraints
- Key personnel with limited CM/GC experience or unclear availability
- Vague risk management ("we will manage risks as they arise")
- Missing or incomplete sections
- Exceeding page limits or ignoring formatting requirements
